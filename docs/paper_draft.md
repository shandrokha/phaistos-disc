### Abstract

The Phaistos Disc (ca. 1700 BCE) carries 241 sign impressions of 45 types, arranged in 61 groups. While Davis (2018) demonstrated statistically (p = 1–3%) that the Disc and Linear A likely encode the same language, he deliberately refrained from assigning phonetic values — the logical next step. We complete that step by applying phonetic values derived from published Disc–Linear A sign correspondences (Daidalika signary, ed. G. Owens, TEI Crete; foundational thesis Owens 2007) and testing the resulting reading against nine Bronze Age language hypotheses using a consistent statistical framework: bigram overlap with Monte Carlo simulation (100,000 iterations per language, seeded PRNG for reproducibility) under both uniform and frequency-weighted null models (specifically, syllables are drawn from those appearing in at least one bigram in either corpus). Eight competing hypotheses — Mycenaean Greek, Luwian, West Semitic, Proto-Finno-Ugric, Middle Egyptian, Hurrian, Etruscan, and Hittite (via Rasmussen's independent sign values) — show no evidence of relationship under this test (uniform p = 0.101–1.000; frequency-weighted p = 0.895–1.000). Only Minoan/Linear A survives under the uniform null (p = 0.032), though the result does not survive Holm-Bonferroni correction for nine simultaneous tests (Holm p = 0.286) nor a frequency-weighted null model (FW p = 0.706). A uniform circularity control confirms that the specific sign correspondences outperform random reshufflings of the same syllable inventory (p = 0.0047), though a frequency-weighted reshuffling control reveals that the positive Minoan overlap is largely attributable to frequency correlation (p = 0.365). A separate leave-PD-inspired-out ablation (§3.4) reveals that 10 of 34 Linear A reference entries (~29%) carry an explicit *cf. PD …* note in the source data and were therefore curated against Phaistos Disc parallels; restricting the comparison to the 24 entries that do not carry such a note drops the bigram overlap from 9 to 5 and raises the uncorrected MC p-value from 0.032 to 0.261, materially weakening the headline Minoan signal. The eight negative results are robust across all null models and corrections. The reading exhibits productive affixation (7 minimal pairs), a *-ti* suffix pattern (13/61 words), and a complementary `°-qe` prefix pattern (13/61 words, where `°` is the sign-02 determinative followed by sign 12 = `qe`), paralleling known Linear A morphology. Sensitivity analyses confirm that the eight negative verdicts are robust to allograph merging and the sign 18 value, and show that under the alternative phonetic treatment of sign 02 (= *i*) the headline Minoan uniform p drifts to 0.056 — i.e. the sole positive verdict is itself sensitive to one annotation choice. All analyses are implemented in an interactive open-source browser workbench (~5,500 lines of application code, including ~2,600 lines of statistical engine) enabling full independent replication. The results represent the first systematic, reproducible multi-language statistical test of a phonetic reading for the Phaistos Disc.

**Keywords**: Phaistos Disc, Linear A, Minoan, undeciphered scripts, Monte Carlo simulation, computational archaeology, Bronze Age Aegean

### 1. Introduction

#### 1.1 The Phaistos Disc and the convergence problem

The Phaistos Disc is a fired clay disc, approximately 16 cm in diameter, bearing 241 sign impressions of 45 distinct types arranged in 61 groups separated by incised dividing lines. The signs were produced with individual stamps — the earliest known use of movable type — and arranged in a spiral on each face. The disc was recovered by Luigi Pernier during the 1908 excavation of the Minoan palace at Phaistos, in southern Crete, from a Middle Minoan III context conventionally dated to approximately 1700 BCE (Pernier 1908; Militello 2000). No second example of the script has been securely identified, making the disc a single-document corpus.

Since its discovery, the disc has attracted over a century of decipherment proposals spanning Greek (Hempl 1911; Faucounau 1975), Luwian (Achterberg, Best & Enzler 2004), Semitic (Aartun 1992), Proto-Finno-Ugric (Revesz 2015), Hittite (Rasmussen 2010), and Minoan (Owens 1999, 2007; Daidalika signary), among others. What distinguishes this history from that of Linear B is its trajectory. Linear B decipherment efforts gradually *converged* toward Ventris's 1952 solution, with independent researchers circling the same phonetic territory over decades. Phaistos Disc proposals have done the opposite: they have *diverged* for 116 years, with each new method producing a different, mutually exclusive reading. This pattern is itself significant. It is not merely evidence of difficulty but a predictable consequence of corpus size.

Barber (1974) proved mathematically that a corpus of 241 tokens drawn from 45 sign types is insufficient to verify any linguistic decipherment by statistical methods. Multiple internally consistent readings can be proposed and none falsified — a property she termed the *decipherment limit*. Any approach that claims to have "read" the disc from internal evidence alone confronts this constraint. Duhoux (2000) sharpened the critique, arguing that the chain of inference from Linear B phonetic values through Linear A to the disc rests on "a stack of unverifiable assumptions" whose conclusions remain "unproven, and unprovable."

A more fundamental challenge has also been raised. Kuykendall (1985, revisited in subsequent online articles, most recently 2024 / 2026) conducted what he describes as the first quantitative positional distribution analysis designed to test whether the disc encodes language at all. His finding — that the disc's 45 sign types partition into three non-overlapping positional classes in a pattern he argues is inconsistent with natural language — raises the possibility that the artifact is a structured data format (e.g. a trade directory) rather than text. We cite the most recent restatement (Kuykendall 2026) where it informs methodological points and the underlying 1985 analysis where it is the original substantive claim. Independently, ten Cate (2011, 2013) demonstrated statistically significant inter-winding correlations (p < 0.05) between signs in adjacent spiral turns, suggesting two-dimensional structure not expected in one-dimensional text. These challenges must be addressed before any phonetic reading can be taken seriously; we engage with them directly in Section 4.

Yet the disc is not entirely opaque. Its internal structure — word-length distributions (Macdonald 1999), alliteration patterns (Ipsen 1929; Timm 2004), prefix-to-suffix ratios (Duhoux 1977), and positional sign distributions — provides constraints that any valid reading must satisfy. The question is whether these constraints, combined with external evidence, can narrow the field of competing hypotheses even if they cannot verify a single one.

The present study adopts this framing explicitly. Our goal is not decipherment in the traditional sense — a complete, verified translation — but *statistical hypothesis elimination*: applying a consistent quantitative test across multiple language candidates and reporting which, if any, survive. We prioritize intellectual honesty: we report what the data shows, including results that weaken our findings; we present only claims the evidence supports; and we consider a null result — that no language hypothesis can be confirmed — a valid and publishable outcome.

#### 1.2 The Davis breakthrough

Davis (2018) introduced a method that fundamentally changed the evidentiary landscape for the Phaistos Disc. His *syllabotactic analysis* compares the positional distributions of *homomorphic signs* — visually similar signs shared between two scripts — without assigning phonetic values to any of them. If two scripts encode the same language, their shared signs should exhibit similar positional behaviour (word-initial, word-internal, word-final) because they are subject to the same phonotactic constraints. The method is elegant precisely because it sidesteps the Barber limit: it does not attempt to read the text, only to characterise the language behind it.

Applied to the Phaistos Disc and Linear A, Davis found that the positional distributions of homomorphic signs match with a probability of p = 0.01–0.03 under the null hypothesis of no linguistic relationship — that is, 97–99% confidence that the two scripts encode the same language. As a control, he applied the same test to Linear B and the Cypriot Syllabary (both known to encode Greek), obtaining 97% agreement, and to Linear A and the Cypriot Syllabary, obtaining only 55% — correctly predicting that Linear A does not encode Greek. The Phaistos Disc–Linear B comparison yielded 45%, confirming independently that the disc does not encode Greek.

Davis's 2026 monograph (*The Undeciphered Aegean Scripts*, Cambridge University Press) extends this analysis in Chapter 8, identifying 14 specific PD–Linear A homomorphs and tabulating their word-internal pair distributions. The 2018 result was recognised with the Michael Ventris Award for Mycenaean Studies in 2019.

Crucially, Davis deliberately refrained from the logical next step. Having established that the disc and Linear A likely encode the same language, he did not assign phonetic values to the disc's signs or test specific language hypotheses. He considered this beyond what the data could support, given the Barber limit. The present study takes that step.

#### 1.3 Our contribution

We complete the inference chain that Davis left open. Using the most comprehensive published table of Phaistos Disc–to–Linear A sign correspondences — the Daidalika signary, compiled and curated by Gareth Owens (TEI Crete / Hellenic Mediterranean University) on the basis of his 2007 doctoral thesis (Owens 2007) and earlier work (Owens 1999), with sign-form scans drawn in part from Coleman's online comparative material — which maps 44 of the disc's 45 signs to Linear A counterparts and thence to Linear B phonetic values, we generate a candidate phonetic reading of the entire disc. We then test this reading against nine Bronze Age language hypotheses using a single, consistent statistical framework. We use "Daidalika signary" throughout this paper to denote the comparative sign table at `daidalika.hmu.gr` rather than as shorthand for any single dated publication; an earlier release of this manuscript inadvertently cited the table as "Owens & Coleman 2017", which conflates the website (no fixed publication year) with Owens's underlying scholarship and overstates Coleman's authorial role; we correct that here.

The framework is as follows. For each candidate language, we compile a reference vocabulary of 34–72 words from published sources, converted to the same CV (consonant-vowel) syllabic notation as the disc reading. We compute the number of shared bigrams (contiguous two-syllable sequences) between the disc reading and the reference vocabulary, then assess significance via Monte Carlo simulation: in each of 100,000 iterations, we generate random "disc-like" text by sampling syllables from the combined syllable pool of the disc and the reference vocabulary (specifically, from syllables appearing in at least one bigram in either corpus) while preserving observed word lengths, and count the resulting bigram overlap. The p-value is the fraction of random iterations that match or exceed the observed overlap. A seeded pseudo-random number generator (mulberry32) ensures exact reproducibility. We supplement the Monte Carlo test with hypergeometric exact p-values as an analytical cross-check.

The nine languages tested are: Minoan/Linear A, Mycenaean Greek, Luwian, West Semitic (Ugaritic/Canaanite), Proto-Finno-Ugric, Middle Egyptian, Hurrian, Etruscan/Tyrsenian, and Hittite (the last via Rasmussen's independent sign-value assignments, providing a second, non-Daidalika reading). This set encompasses every serious published proposal for the disc's language and extends coverage to plausible but untested candidates from the Bronze Age eastern Mediterranean.

The principal result is that eight of nine language hypotheses yield non-significant results under this test. Mycenaean Greek, Luwian, West Semitic, Proto-Finno-Ugric, Middle Egyptian, Hurrian, Etruscan, and Hittite all fail the same test under both a uniform null (p = 0.101–1.000) and a frequency-weighted null where syllables are sampled in proportion to their combined corpus frequency (FW p = 0.895–1.000). Only Minoan/Linear A survives under the uniform null (Monte Carlo p = 0.032, α = 0.05), though this result does not survive Holm-Bonferroni correction for nine simultaneous tests (Holm p = 0.286) and is not significant under the frequency-weighted null (FW p = 0.706). We address the circularity inherent in testing a Linear A–derived reading against Linear A at two further levels. Randomly reshuffling the same 43 Daidalika-derived syllable values across sign positions 100,000 times, only 0.47% of reshufflings match or exceed the real overlap (uniform circularity p = 0.0047), confirming that the specific correspondences, not merely the shared syllable pool, drive the result. However, because visual-similarity mapping correlates PD sign frequency with LA sign frequency (Spearman rho = 0.257, computed on rank vectors with the tie-aware Pearson formulation), reshuffling only within matching frequency tiers raises the null mean from 3.2 to 7.8 bigrams and the real overlap of 9 is no longer significant (frequency-weighted circularity p = 0.365). The eight non-significant results, critically, are robust across all null models and corrections, since those languages were not used to derive the values. The reading also exhibits productive affixation (seven minimal pairs, a *°-qe* prefix appearing in 13 of 61 words, a *-ti* suffix in 13 of 61) and a Zipfian frequency distribution consistent with natural language (though Zipfian distributions are necessary but not sufficient evidence for language; Li 1992). Sensitivity analyses confirm robustness of the eight negative verdicts to the treatment of sign 02 (determinative versus phonetic), allograph merging (three confirmed pairs reducing the signary from 45 to 42 types), and the phonetic value of sign 18 (TI versus RJU); however, under the alternative phonetic treatment of sign 02 (= *i*) the headline Minoan uniform p drifts to 0.056 — close enough to α = 0.05 that the sole positive verdict cannot be claimed to be robust to that single annotation choice.

Three features of this study distinguish it from prior work.

First, the same statistical test is applied consistently across all nine language candidates, enabling direct comparison. Previous studies tested individual language hypotheses in isolation, each with its own methodology, making cross-comparison impossible.

Second, every analysis is implemented in open-source JavaScript, published as an interactive browser workbench with modular engine, worker-based Monte Carlo simulations, and pre-computed defaults that users can re-run live. The engine code (comparison, statistics, phonetics, parsing) totals approximately 2,600 lines; the full application, including presentation and build tooling, approximately 5,500 lines. The entire pipeline — from raw transcription to final p-values — is deterministic and independently reproducible via seeded PRNGs. We are aware of no prior Phaistos Disc study that provides this level of computational transparency.

Third, we explicitly acknowledge the Barber limit throughout and address the circularity inherent in testing a Linear A–derived reading against Linear A. We do not claim to have deciphered the disc or confirmed its language. We claim that eight competing hypotheses are not supported by this test under both uniform and frequency-weighted null models; that the sole survivor — Minoan/Linear A — survives the uniform language-comparison test, though it does not survive multiple-testing correction or the frequency-weighted null; and that these results are reproducible. What we have achieved is hypothesis elimination, not proof of a reading — the first systematic, reproducible multi-language statistical test of a phonetic reading for the Phaistos Disc.

The remainder of the paper is structured as follows. Section 2 describes the data sources, phonetic value assignment procedure, validation checks, and statistical methodology. Section 3 presents the nine-language comparison results, supporting evidence for the Minoan hypothesis, discriminating evidence against competitors, and sensitivity analyses. Section 4 discusses the relationship to Davis's and Owens's work, the Barber limit and what can legitimately be claimed, limitations, and future directions. Section 5 concludes.

### 2. Data and Methods

#### 2.1 Transcription and sign inventory

The analysis begins from a transcription of the Phaistos Disc using Evans's (1909) sign numbering system, which assigns a unique integer (01–45) to each distinct sign type. The transcription follows the conventional segmentation into 31 word-groups on Side A and 30 on Side B, yielding 241 readable sign impressions. (Some sources report a total of 242 stamped impressions, counting a partially damaged sign at position A-VIII-5 whose true value is unrecoverable. Following Godart 1995 and Davis 2018, we exclude that damaged impression from all phonetic and statistical computations, giving 241 readable tokens. The distinction does not affect any statistical result.) Word boundaries are defined by the incised dividing lines visible on the disc's surface.

We verified the transcription against two independent structural analyses. First, we replicated Table 1 of Macdonald (1999), which reports the frequency distribution of word lengths: 6 two-sign words, 16 three-sign words, 21 four-sign words, and 18 words of five to seven signs. Our transcription reproduces these counts exactly. Second, we replicated the alliteration patterns identified by Timm (2004): consecutive word-groups sharing the same initial sign (e.g., sign 29 in Side A fields III–IV, sign 02 in fields XVI–XVII, XIX–XX, XXII–XXIII) and consecutive word-groups sharing the same final sign (e.g., sign 35 in Side A fields IX–X). All published patterns are reproduced. These checks serve as regression tests: any subsequent change to the transcription or parsing logic must continue to pass them.

Reading direction follows the prevailing scholarly consensus: outside-to-centre, with signs read left-to-right within each spiral segment (Duhoux 1977; Olivier 1975; Godart 1995; Baldacci 2024). Our analysis includes a directionality sensitivity sweep — reversing word order, reversing sign order within words, or both — to confirm that structural claims are robust to this assumption.

One word-group (B XIII) contains an internal marker (^) whose function is unclear; it is discarded during parsing and does not affect any statistical test.

#### 2.2 Phonetic value assignment

Phonetic values are assigned to the disc's signs using the Daidalika signary (curated by G. Owens, TEI Crete / Hellenic Mediterranean University; see Owens 1999, 2007 for the underlying scholarship), a comparison table that maps each Phaistos Disc sign to a proposed Linear A counterpart on the basis of visual similarity. Because a substantial subset of Linear A signs share their forms with Linear B — a deciphered script — phonetic values can be inferred through the chain PD → LA → LB. This chain of inference, while well-established for certain signs, accumulates uncertainty at each step (Duhoux 2000).

Of the disc's 45 sign types, 44 receive a proposed Linear A correspondence. The remaining sign (the oblique stroke) is a modifier, not a syllabogram. Among the 44, we assign candidate phonetic values to 43 signs. Sign 02 ("plumed head") is treated as a non-phonetic determinative rather than the vowel *i* proposed by Owens. This treatment is motivated by its unique distributional properties: sign 02 occurs 19 times, exclusively in word-initial position (19/19), a pattern unmatched by any other sign and statistically anomalous under a free-combination binomial null with the empirical word-initial probability (61/241 ≈ 0.253), giving `(0.253)^19 ≈ 1.2 × 10⁻¹²`, well below 10⁻⁶ as a conservative bound. The same pattern is also reproduced in our Kuykendall mutual-information replication (Section 4.4). Kuykendall's (2026) own distributional analysis independently supports the determinative treatment.

Each correspondence is assigned a confidence level:

| Confidence | Count | Criterion |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| High | 4 signs | Multiple independent sources agree (Evans, Younger, Davis, Daidalika) |
| Medium | 14 signs | Daidalika proposes; at least one independent corroboration |
| Lower | 25 signs | Daidalika proposes; no independent corroboration or conflict |
| None | 2 signs | No phonetic value assigned (sign 02 as determinative; sign 17 has no established value) |

The four high-confidence correspondences are: PD12 → AB78 QE, PD19 → AB31 SA, PD29 → AB80 MA, and PD31 → AB81 KU. These serve as seed values for the Ventris-style consonant–vowel grid described below.

Three pairs of signs share the same Linear A mapping: PD07/PD18 → AB37 (TI), PD19/PD33 → AB31 (SA), and PD26/PD45 → AB76 (RJA). Deep distributional analysis (cosine similarity of positional vectors) suggests that PD07/PD18 are conditioned allographs (cosine = 0.885; PD07 appears in all positions, PD18 never initially), PD19/PD33 are likely distinct signs despite sharing AB31 (cosine = 0.598), and PD26/PD45 show moderate positional complementarity (cosine = 0.693). Sensitivity analyses (Section 2.7) confirm that merging or splitting these pairs does not affect the principal results.

Two corrections were applied during research: sign 26 was reassigned from AB77 KA to AB76 RJA, and sign 38 from AB38 E to AB77 KA, based on re-examination of the Daidalika signary and cross-referencing with Owens's published transliterations. Both corrections are documented in the analysis script for transparency.

#### 2.3 Validation of the phonetic assignment

Before proceeding to language comparison, we validate the phonetic assignment against several internal consistency checks that any genuine syllabary should satisfy.

**Syllable structure.** All 241 sign impressions, when converted to phonetic values, produce either CV (consonant–vowel) or V (pure vowel) syllables. No CVC, CCV, or other non-standard structures appear. (Note: since the Daidalika signary assigns exclusively CV or V values, this consistency is partly a consequence of the input design rather than an independent validation. It does confirm that no sign-assignment error introduced non-CV syllables.) We note that this 100% CV/V consistency is a *necessary consequence* of deriving values from Linear B, which is itself a strictly CV/V system: any mapping from PD signs to LB-derived values will produce CV/V output regardless of whether the mapping is correct. The check therefore does not provide independent evidence for the reading's validity; rather, it confirms that no errors were introduced during the derivation chain (e.g., mistranscribed sign numbers or garbled phonetic values). A CVC or CCV output would signal a pipeline error, not a linguistic finding.

**Positional consistency.** Every sign appears in exactly those word positions (initial, medial, final) where it is independently observed in the disc's positional profile. No sign is forced into an unattested position by the phonetic reading.

**Phonotactic plausibility.** The resulting syllable frequency distribution is consistent with Zipf's law (log-log OLS slope ≈ −1.0, Pearson r = −0.93), though confidence intervals on the slope are wide (approximately ±0.2) given only 43 distinct syllable types from 241 tokens. The vowel hierarchy (a > i > e > u > o) matches typological expectations for Aegean languages. Pure vowels (syllables with no consonant onset) constitute approximately 9% of tokens, within the expected 5–15% range for CV syllabaries (Linear B: ~12%). We note, following Li (1992), that Zipfian distributions are a necessary but not sufficient condition for natural language, since certain classes of random processes also produce them.

**Ventris-style grid.** Grouping the 43 phonetic values by shared consonant and vowel produces a 19 × 5 (consonant × vowel) grid. Signs sharing a consonant but differing in vowel exhibit correlated positional distributions, as expected if they represent syllables of the same consonant class.

**Morphological productivity (structural-script evidence).** The reading reveals seven minimal pairs — word-forms differing by exactly one syllable in one position — suggesting productive morphological processes. The seven pairs are: (1) *ma-rja-ti* ↔ *ti-rja-ti* (A III / B XXIV; initial ma ↔ ti); (2) *wi-rja-ti-qe* ↔ *wi-rja-ti-te* (A VI / B II; final qe ↔ te); (3) *ku-rja-te* ↔ *ku-rja-qe* (A IX / A XXV; final te ↔ qe); (4) *re-je* ↔ *pa-je* (A XV / A XXX; initial re ↔ pa); (5) *re-je* ↔ *pa-je* (A XXI / A XXX; same frame, different attestation); (6) *ma-rja-ti* ↔ *ti-rja-ti* (B XX / B XXIV; same frame as pair 1, Side B attestation); (7) *wi-pi-na-dwa* ↔ *ma-pi-na-dwa* (B XXII / B XXIX; initial wi ↔ ma). Pairs 2–3 exhibit a recurring *te* ↔ *qe* alternation in independent word frames, suggesting paradigmatic morphological variation. Note that pairs 4 and 5 collapse to a single unique frame, as do pairs 1 and 6; the seven attested contrasts therefore reduce to five unique phonetic frames. Two-syllable pairs (frame 4/5) are evidentially weaker than 3- and 4-syllable pairs because substituting one syllable in a two-syllable word leaves only 50% of the form intact — a higher chance baseline than in longer words. A *°-qe* prefix appears in 13 of 61 words (21%), always immediately following the sign 02 determinative, consistent with a grammatical particle or case marker. A *-ti* suffix appears in 13 of 61 words (21%), paralleling the *-ti* ending well-attested in Linear A (e.g., *u-na-ru-ka-na-ti*, PK Za 11).

**Important scope clarification.** The existence of seven minimal pairs, the *°-qe* prefix count, the *-ti* suffix count, and the Sign 02 / Sign 12 binding (Section 4.5) are all properties of the *raw sign sequences*, not of the Daidalika phonetic assignment. Because the assignment is approximately a 1-to-1 sign-to-syllable map (with three documented allograph pairs), any alternative bijection from the 43 phonetic signs to 43 distinct phonetic values would produce the *same* number of minimal pairs, the *same* prefix and suffix counts (with relabelled phonetic values), and the *same* Sign 02 / Sign 12 contingency. These features therefore support the hypothesis that the disc encodes a *structured linguistic text* — directly relevant to the linguistic-vs-non-linguistic debate (Kuykendall 1985; Section 4.4) — but they do not by themselves validate the specific Daidalika phonetic values. The only morphological feature whose evidential value depends on the phonetic assignment is the *phonetic plausibility* of the substitutions in minimal pairs: the workbench's `minimalPairPlausibility` test reports that of the five unique minimal-pair frames, only one (*te* ↔ *qe*) shares a phonetic feature (the vowel /e/); the others (*ma* ↔ *ti*, *ma* ↔ *wi*, *re* ↔ *pa*) do not, a result statistically indistinguishable from chance given the sign inventory. We therefore frame Section 2.3 as evidence that *something linguistic* is being encoded, not as independent validation of the Daidalika reading. The directionality sensitivity sweep tests bigram overlap and baseline alignment under reading-direction variants; morphological patterns (minimal pairs, affix productivity) have not been independently tested under reversed reading.

#### 2.4 Language comparison methodology

The central statistical test compares the phonetic reading of the disc against reference vocabularies for each candidate language. We note at the outset that our method — set intersection of distinct bigrams — departs from the standard computational language identification literature, which typically uses n-gram frequency ranking (Cavnar and Trenkle 1994), cosine distance between frequency vectors, or machine-learning classifiers. Those methods are designed for texts of hundreds or thousands of characters in known modern scripts. The Phaistos Disc presents a fundamentally different problem: a 241-token corpus in a possibly unique script, tested against fragmentary reference vocabularies of 34–72 words. With so few data points, frequency-based distance metrics would be dominated by noise. Set intersection of distinct bigrams is a deliberately coarse statistic that maximises robustness at the expense of discriminatory power — a conservative choice appropriate for the corpus size. We report both uniform and frequency-weighted null models to bound the sensitivity to this design decision.

**Bigram overlap.** For each candidate language, we compile a reference vocabulary of published words converted to CV syllabic notation (see Section 2.5). We compute the set of distinct bigrams — contiguous two-syllable pairs — appearing in both the disc reading and the reference vocabulary. The number of shared bigrams is the test statistic.

**Monte Carlo null model.** To assess significance, we generate random "disc-like" texts under the null hypothesis that the disc and the reference vocabulary are unrelated. We report results under two sampling models. Under the *uniform null* (primary), each of 100,000 iterations generates synthetic words by drawing syllables uniformly at random from the combined syllable pool of the disc and the reference vocabulary (specifically, from syllables appearing in at least one bigram in either corpus), preserving the observed word lengths from the disc. Under the *frequency-weighted null* (conservative), syllables are drawn in proportion to their token frequency in the combined PD and reference corpus, producing synthetic text with more realistic frequency structure. For each model, the p-value is the fraction of iterations in which the synthetic overlap meets or exceeds the observed overlap.

Both null models draw syllables from the *combined* pool rather than only the disc's pool, giving the null hypothesis access to the reference language's syllables and thus maximising the chance of accidental overlap. Both preserve the disc's word-length distribution. A design note: the syllable pool is constructed from syllables appearing in at least one bigram (i.e., from words of 2+ syllables); syllables found exclusively in single-syllable words are excluded. This slightly reduces the pool size, making random collisions more likely and the test more conservative — our reported p-values are, if anything, overestimates relative to a full-unigram pool. The frequency-weighted model is more linguistically realistic — natural languages do not use syllables uniformly — but substantially more conservative. Seeded pseudo-random number generators (mulberry32; seed 314159 for the uniform language-comparison null, seed 271828 for the frequency-weighted language-comparison null) ensure exact reproducibility across runs and platforms. Circularity bias controls (Section 2.6) use seed 414159 for uniform reshuffling and seed 414160 for frequency-weighted reshuffling (independent stream: base seed + 1). Each language uses a per-language seed offset (seed + language index, 0-indexed in Table 3 order), ensuring independent PRNG streams across languages. Separate seeds prevent the frequency-weighted iterations from shifting the uniform PRNG state, preserving backward compatibility. Mulberry32 is a 32-bit PRNG that passes gjrand's 13-test suite (P = 0.984) and is adequate for Monte Carlo estimation at 10⁵–10⁶ iterations, though it cannot produce approximately one-third of all possible 32-bit outputs (Ettinger, personal communication). In JavaScript, the implementation uses `| 0` coercion to avoid floating-point precision loss after ~3.4 million iterations. For the present application, where the critical property is run-to-run determinism rather than cryptographic quality, mulberry32 is sufficient; we verified that switching to the xoshiro128** PRNG produces p-values within ±0.002 of those reported here.

**Hypergeometric cross-check.** As an analytical supplement, we compute a hypergeometric p-value treating the overlap as a sampling problem: given *N* possible bigrams in the combined syllable space, *K* bigrams in the reference vocabulary, and *n* bigrams in the disc reading, the probability of observing *k* or more shared bigrams by chance. The hypergeometric model assumes independence among bigrams (a simplification that the Monte Carlo model avoids by preserving word structure) and thus tends toward smaller p-values. Agreement between the two methods strengthens confidence in the result; disagreement flags cases where word-structure effects matter.

Languages 1–8 are tested using the Daidalika-derived phonetic reading. Hittite is tested separately using Rasmussen's (2010) independent sign-value assignments, providing a control that uses a completely different reading of the disc. Reference vocabularies range from 34 to 72 words; smaller corpora (Minoan, Etruscan) reflect the limited attestation of these languages in CV syllabic form. The four primary corpora — Linear A, Mycenaean Greek, Luwian, and West Semitic — carry per-entry source citations (specific tablets, inscriptions, or corpus identifiers) in the underlying data file (`data.mjs`). The five secondary corpora — Proto-Finno-Ugric, Middle Egyptian, Hurrian, Etruscan/Tyrsenian, and Hittite — are drawn from the standard reference grammars and lexica listed in this section without per-entry tablet-level citations; each entry instead carries a glossing note (and, for PFU, a reconstructed proto-form) tying it to its source family. This citation asymmetry reflects the differing attestation density across these corpora and is documented here for transparency.

Reference vocabulary sizes vary from 34 to 72 words, and smaller corpora shift both the observed overlap and the null distribution in ways that are not straightforwardly comparable. A formal power analysis per language, or equalization by subsampling all vocabularies to 34 words, would strengthen the cross-language ranking and is available in the workbench's Extended Analysis section (subsampling each corpus to 34 words across 50 trials). The current results should therefore be interpreted as language-specific tests rather than a strict ordinal ranking.

Semitic, Proto-Finno-Ugric, and Etruscan words are converted to CV notation following Linear B conventions: /l/ is mapped to the r-series (no distinct l-series exists in the Linear B syllabary), and consonant clusters are simplified to attested CV patterns (e.g., /lm/ → /m/, dropping the first consonant). The /l/ → r-series rule is applied to every Semitic and Etruscan entry; PFU has two retained-/l/ exceptions, both for collision-avoidance reasons: PFU `*kala` "fish" already occupies `ka-ra`, so `*kälä` "tongue" is left as `ka-la` to avoid silently merging two distinct lexemes; and `*neljä` "four" is left as `ne-la` for parallel reasons. Both retained-/l/ syllables (`la`, `ne-la`) fail to match any Phaistos Disc bigram under the Daidalika reading, so the headline numbers are unaffected by these exceptions; they are documented here for full corpus reproducibility. Hittite, Greek, Luwian, Hurrian, Egyptian, and Minoan use their own attested transliteration conventions (Hittite via Rasmussen 2017's Latin transliteration; Greek/Linear A via the standard Linear B/A syllabary; Luwian via the cuneiform-Hieroglyphic transliteration tradition; Hurrian via the Mitanni-letter syllabary; Egyptian via Middle Egyptian transliteration) and do not receive the /l/ → r substitution.

The Minoan reference vocabulary (34 words) is curated from a larger attested Linear A corpus of 49 forms by excluding place names (*a-mi-ni-so*, *ko-no-so*, *pa-i-to*) that may be pre-Minoan substrate borrowings, and forms with uncertain or fragmentary readings and probable Greek borrowings (e.g., *te-me-no*, cf. Greek τέμενος) that would inflate Minoan overlap. This conservative curation reduces potential false-positive overlap at the cost of statistical power. The workbench's Extended Analysis section confirms that including all 49 forms increases Minoan overlap from 9 to 10 (MC p ≈ 0.0305), indicating the curation is conservative rather than cherry-picked.

The choice of languages is motivated by three criteria: (a) the language has been proposed in the literature as a candidate for the Phaistos Disc, (b) the language was geographically and temporally plausible as a contact language in Bronze Age Crete, or (c) the language provides a typological contrast (e.g., Egyptian as an isolating language, Proto-Finno-Ugric as an agglutinative language with suffix-only morphology).

#### 2.6 Circularity bias controls

Because the Daidalika signary derives phonetic values from Linear A, testing the resulting reading against Linear A vocabulary introduces circularity. The concern is not trivial: if the shared syllable inventory between the disc reading and the reference vocabulary is large enough, some bigram overlap will arise by chance regardless of whether the specific sign assignments are correct. We address this concern at two levels.

**Level 1: uniform reshuffling.** We retain the same set of 43 Daidalika-derived syllable values but randomly reassign them to different sign positions. For each of 100,000 reshufflings, we generate a new disc reading, compute its Minoan bigram overlap, and record the result. If the real assignment's overlap is unremarkable among the reshuffled assignments, the result is an artifact of the shared syllable inventory. If it ranks in roughly the top 0.47% of permutations (470 of 100,000), the specific sign correspondences carry genuine signal beyond the pool itself.

**Level 2: frequency-weighted reshuffling.** Visual-similarity mapping may introduce a second confound. Because researchers are more likely to notice and match signs that are common in both scripts, the Daidalika signary may pair frequent PD signs with frequent LA syllable values. To test this, we compute the Spearman rank correlation between each sign's disc token frequency and its assigned syllable's frequency in the LA reference vocabulary. We then divide signs and values into three frequency tiers (high, medium, low) and reshuffle values only within matching tiers, preserving the macro frequency structure while randomizing specific identities. This test isolates the contribution of frequency correlation from that of specific sign assignments.

#### 2.7 Sensitivity analyses

Three parameters of the analysis involve choices that could plausibly affect the results. We test each by running the full nine-language comparison under both settings and comparing outcomes.

**Sign 02 treatment.** Our default analysis treats sign 02 as a determinative, excluding it from the phonetic text. The alternative is Owens's reading as the vowel *i*. Under the alternative, sign 02 contributes additional syllable tokens (19 instances of *i*) and potentially new bigrams.

**Allograph merging.** Three sign pairs (PD07/PD18, PD19/PD33, PD26/PD45) share the same Daidalika-assigned phonetic value. Merging each pair into a single sign type reduces the signary from 45 to 42 types. The test asks whether this reduction affects bigram counts or p-values.

**Sign 18 value.** The Daidalika signary assigns PD18 the value TI (the same as PD07). Owens (1999) and Palmer read PD18 as RJU. We test both values.

All sensitivity analyses are implemented in the published browser workbench. The interactive UI presents default and alternative results side-by-side and supports live Monte Carlo re-computation with user-adjustable iteration counts.

#### 3.2 Supporting evidence for Minoan

Beyond the aggregate bigram statistic, the Minoan comparison reveals qualitative patterns consistent with a genuine linguistic relationship.

**Word-level parallels.** The disc reading shares 17 word-level parallels with Linear A, defined as disc word-groups sharing two or more contiguous syllables with an attested Linear A word. Among the strongest:

- *wi-pi-na-dwa* (B XXII) shares the *pi-na* core with LA *i-pi-na-ma* (IO Za 2, PK Za 11), a libation formula term glossed as "I distribute."
- *qe-ku-rja* (A XVI, A XIX, A XXII) shares the *qe-ku* core with LA *qe-ku-re* (attested at multiple sites), a term tentatively glossed as "underground."
- *je-si-ru-ti* (A XI) shares the *si-ru* core with LA *si-ru-te* (IO Za 2), a libation formula term.
- *ti-ti-te* (B XXIII) under the RJU reading of sign 18 becomes *ti-rju-te*, matching the *ru-te* ending of LA *si-ru-te* (IO Za 2) and *pi-rju-te* (HT 116a.4).

All 17 parallels share exactly two contiguous syllables; no three-syllable matches were found, consistent with the small reference corpus (34 words).

**Morphological compatibility.** Of 12 known Linear A suffixes tested, 8 appear in the disc reading: *-ti* (13 words), *-te* (7 words), *-sa* (4 words), *-ma* (3 words), *-ja* (2 words), *-no* (2 words), *-na-ti* (1 word), and *-ru-te* (1 word). Of 5 known Linear A prefixes, 2 appear: *ja-* and *ma-*. The productive *-ti* suffix (13/61 words, 21%) parallels the *-ti* ending attested across Linear A texts (e.g., *u-na-ru-ka-na-ti*, PK Za 11; *ti-ti-ku*, HT 86a), where it is widely interpreted as a grammatical morpheme.

Two independent minimal pairs show a *te* ↔ *qe* alternation in the same word frame, suggesting paradigmatic morphological variation rather than free lexical substitution. This is consistent with the behaviour of an inflectional system.

#### 3.3 Discriminating evidence against competitors

Each language that is not supported by this test exhibits specific diagnostic incompatibilities with the disc reading.

**Mycenaean Greek** (p = 0.6362). Zero three-syllable matches. Eight diagnostic Mycenaean patterns — *me-no* (participial), *re-u* (masculine ending), *jo* (relative pronoun), *si* (sibilant prefix), *to-so* ("so much"), *wa-na-ka* ("king"), *po-ti-ni-ja* ("mistress"), *ti-ri-po* ("tripod") — are entirely absent from the disc reading. The Greek hypothesis requires the disc to lack every characteristic Mycenaean feature while also not being supported by the bigram test under any of our nulls.

**Luwian** (p = 0.1765). Approximately 14% of the Luwian reference vocabulary contains h-series syllables (ha, hi, hu, etc.), reflecting the language's characteristic h-initial morphemes (*hassu-* "king," *hawi-* "sheep," *hanti* "front"). The Daidalika signary assigns no h-series value to any disc sign, creating a systematic phonological gap. This gap is not merely a chance absence: it reflects the absence of the phoneme /h/ in the Minoan syllable inventory, which would be unexpected if the disc encoded Luwian.

**West Semitic** (p = 0.9880). The disc reading exhibits exclusively affixing morphology (prefixes and suffixes modifying a stem), while West Semitic languages employ triconsonantal root-and-pattern morphology, where meaning is encoded in the consonant skeleton and grammatical relations in the vowel pattern. When Semitic words are forced into a CV syllabary (as the El-Amarna scribes did), the resulting patterns are distinctive: *Ca-Cv-Cv* verb forms with echo vowels, a high frequency of *-Cu* endings, and systematic h/ḥ/ḫ consonant usage. None of these patterns appear in the disc reading.

**Proto-Finno-Ugric** (p = 0.7188). PFU is a strictly suffix-only language: all grammatical modification occurs through suffixes, with no productive prefixes. The disc reading shows productive prefixation: *°-qe* appears as a prefix in 13 of 61 words (21%), *°-ma* in 8 words (13%). This prefix system is typologically incompatible with PFU under the assumption that the disc's word-formation conventions are CV-syllabary-typical.

**Middle Egyptian** (p = 1.000) and **Hurrian** (p = 0.8102). Both languages rely heavily on h-series consonants (Egyptian: approximately 30% of vocabulary; Hurrian: approximately 18%) and both produce zero or near-zero bigram overlap. The disc reading's absence of h-series values, combined with its CV-only syllable structure and affixing morphology, is inconsistent with both languages under this operationalization.

**Etruscan/Tyrsenian** (p = 1.000). Zero shared bigrams despite Etruscan's geographic proximity to the Aegean (Lemnos). The Tyrsenian syllable inventory is sufficiently distinct from the Minoan-derived reading that no vocabulary overlap arises.

#### 3.4 Circularity bias controls

**Uniform reshuffling.** When the same 43 Daidalika-derived syllable values are randomly reassigned to different sign positions (100,000 iterations), the mean overlap drops to 3.16 bigrams, with a 95th percentile of 6 and a 99th percentile of 8. The real overlap of 9 exceeds both thresholds: only 470 of 100,000 reshufflings match or exceed 9 (uniform circularity p = 0.0047, 0.47%). This confirms that the specific sign correspondences produce more overlap than random assignments drawn from the same syllable pool.

<!-- historical --> **Frequency-weighted reshuffling.** The Daidalika signary exhibits a mild positive correlation between disc sign frequency and the assigned syllable's frequency in the LA reference vocabulary (Spearman rho = 0.257, computed as Pearson correlation on rank vectors so that ties in the LA syllable-frequency distribution are handled correctly; an earlier release used the no-ties closed form 1 − 6 ΣD²/(n(n²−1)) and reported 0.297, which is biased upward in the presence of ties — the corrected value is reported here). This is expected: visual-similarity mapping is more likely to succeed for common signs, which are better attested in both scripts. To isolate the contribution of frequency matching from that of specific sign identities, we divide signs into three frequency tiers (high, medium, low: 15, 15, and 13 signs respectively) and reshuffle values only within matching tiers. Under this frequency-preserved null, the mean overlap rises to 7.79 bigrams — close to the real 9. Of 100,000 iterations, 35,941 match or exceed 9 (frequency-weighted circularity p = 0.359). A minor technical caveat: three allograph pairs share identical phonetic values (PD07/18→ti, PD19/33→sa, PD26/45→rja); when both members of a pair fall in the same frequency tier, some reshufflings are effectively no-ops, marginally reducing the number of truly distinct configurations tested.

A second caveat applies to the *framing* of the frequency-weighted null. Because the per-syllable frequencies that drive the FW sampler are computed from the union of PD-reading bigrams and LA-reference bigrams, the null inherits the empirical frequency profile of the reading itself. The FW null is therefore best understood as "how much overlap survives when we destroy *which value sits on which sign* but preserve *the joint frequency landscape that the PD reading and the LA corpus produce when combined*", not as a hypothesis-free baseline. We retain the FW null as a conservative diagnostic but stop short of treating its p-value as the principal test.

**Interpretation.** The uniform test rejects the hypothesis that any assignment from the same syllable pool would work. The frequency-weighted test reveals that much of the remaining signal — approximately 5 of the 9 shared bigrams — is attributable to frequency correlation: frequent disc signs being paired with frequent LA values. The specific identity of each correspondence, beyond its frequency rank, adds approximately 2 additional bigrams but is not independently significant.

**Linear A reference-vocabulary curation overlap (leave-PD-inspired-out ablation).** A separate, stronger form of circularity arises from the *Linear A reference vocabulary itself*. Of the 34 entries in the project's `LINEAR_A_WORDS` data file, 10 (~29%) carry an explicit `cf. PD …` note in the source data, indicating that those entries were curated against Phaistos Disc parallels: the listed entries are *si-ru-te*, *ta-na-ra-te*, *u-na-ru-ka-na-ti*, *ma-te-re-sa*, *ti-di-te*, *na-da-re*, *pi-rju-te*, *ja-sa-ja*, *si-da-te*, *ku-ra*. Testing a PD-derived reading against an LA vocabulary that was itself partly built by reading the disc is a stronger form of circularity than the within-mapping reshuffle above: the within-mapping test corrects for *which value gets assigned to which sign*, but it cannot correct for *which words were included in the comparandum*. We therefore run a leave-PD-inspired-out ablation that re-runs the Minoan / LA bigram-overlap MC against the 24 LA entries that do not carry such a note. The result: the bigram overlap drops from 9 to 5, and the uncorrected MC p-value rises from 0.032 to 0.261 (engine output at 100,000 iterations; identifier `leavePdInspiredOut` in the workbench Extended Analysis section #29). Four of the nine shared bigrams therefore depend on PD-curated LA entries. We report this finding prominently because it materially weakens the headline Minoan p-value: the result is exploratory and partly contingent on LA-corpus curation.

Two methodological caveats on this ablation should be stated explicitly. (i) Removing 10 of 34 LA entries reduces the LA reference corpus by ~29%, and the resulting decrease in shared bigrams partly reflects this corpus-size reduction rather than purely the loss of curated parallels. The cleanest way to separate the two effects is to re-curate the LA list from a primary source under preregistered inclusion rules; we list that in §6 as a deferred analysis. (ii) Several short LA entries (e.g., *ku-ra*, *qe-ku-re*) generated multiple of the original nine shared bigrams, so the ablation is dominated by a small number of high-leverage entries rather than a uniform 29% reduction. Both caveats are properly resolved only by the re-curation analysis above.

This does not invalidate the principal result. The eight non-significant results are entirely unaffected by either circularity test, since those languages were not used to derive the phonetic values. The frequency correlation itself is consistent with a shared script tradition — correlated sign frequencies arise naturally when two scripts descend from a common ancestor or when one is adapted from the other. However, the combined effect of the frequency-weighted circularity result, the Holm correction, and the leave-PD-inspired-out ablation is that the positive Minoan overlap should be interpreted as "the sole surviving hypothesis after eight negative results under this test," not as independently confirmed through any of the circularity controls.

#### 3.5 Random reading baseline

As an additional circularity control, we generated random phonetic mappings by drawing syllable values from the full pool of all attested syllables across all nine reference languages. Of the mappings tested, 0% produced Minoan overlap equal to or exceeding the observed 9 shared bigrams. This confirms that the result depends on the specific sign-value assignments, not on any property of the testing framework.

#### 3.6 Sensitivity analyses

**Sign 02 treatment.** Under the default determinative treatment, the Minoan Monte Carlo p-value is 0.032. Under the alternative phonetic treatment (sign 02 = *i*), the p-value rises to 0.056 (exact: 0.0562 at 100K iterations) — *above* α = 0.05. The headline Minoan signal therefore *fails* the uniform language-comparison test under the phonetic treatment of sign 02: the sole positive verdict in the whole nine-language sweep is sensitive to a single annotation choice. This sensitivity is reported here, in the abstract, and in §1.3 because we consider it material: the determinative treatment is supported by sign 02's exclusively word-initial distribution and by Kuykendall's (2026) independent positional analysis, but it is an editorial decision that the Daidalika source itself does not directly endorse. The determinative treatment excludes 19 sign tokens from the phonetic text, reducing the effective corpus from 241 to 222 tokens. No verdict changes for any of the *eight* non-Minoan languages under either treatment.

**Allograph merging.** Merging the three allograph pairs (PD07/PD18, PD19/PD33, PD26/PD45) into single signs collapses the signary from 45 to 42 types. The resulting change in the Minoan p-value is negligible <!-- canonical-exempt:start -->(Δp ≈ 0.000)<!-- canonical-exempt:end -->. This is expected: merging allographs reduces sign-type count but does not change the actual bigram sequences, since the merged signs already share phonetic values.

**Sign 18 value.** Under the Daidalika table value TI, the Minoan p-value is 0.032. Under Owens's/Palmer's RJU reading, the p-value is 0.031 — effectively identical, with no verdict change for any language. The RJU reading produces additional LA bigram matches (e.g., *rju-te* matching LA *pi-rju-te*), consistent with Owens's preference. Both readings support the Minoan hypothesis.

The allograph-merge and sign-18 analyses confirm that the eight negative verdicts and the *direction* of the Minoan signal are robust to reasonable parameter choices. The sign-02 analysis, in contrast, places the Minoan headline result on a knife edge.

#### 3.7 Davis replication

To complement the bigram overlap test, we replicate Davis's (2018) syllabotactic analysis using our Daidalika-derived correspondence set.

**Method.** For each pair of homomorphic signs (PD sign ↔ LA sign sharing the same AB number), we record whether they co-occur as word-internal pairs — that is, whether both signs appear within the same word in their respective scripts. We then count the number of shared word-internal pairs across the disc and the Linear A corpus. Significance is assessed by permutation: randomly relabelling the PD–LA sign correspondences and counting how often the reshuffled pairs match or exceed the real count.

**Results.** Using the full set of 23 homomorphs (all confidence levels above "low"), we observe 17 shared word-internal pairs (permutation <!-- canonical-exempt:start -->p = 0.129<!-- canonical-exempt:end -->). Restricting to the 15 high- and medium-confidence homomorphs, we observe 10 shared pairs (<!-- canonical-exempt:start -->p = 0.116<!-- canonical-exempt:end -->). Positional agreement — the fraction of shared signs appearing in the same word position (initial, medial, or final) in both scripts — is 13/19 = 68%, against a chance expectation of approximately 50%. *(The two permutation p-values are paper-reported auxiliary results from an internal Davis-replication CLI script; they are not currently reproducible by the public workbench. They are listed in §6 ("Deferred reproducibility — paper-cited values not yet exposed") so that a future version of the workbench can expose them under canonical control.)*

**Comparison with Davis.** Davis (2018) reported p = 0.01–0.03 using 14 carefully selected homomorphs. Our higher p-values likely reflect three factors: (a) our larger homomorph set dilutes precision with lower-confidence correspondences; (b) our Linear A corpus (34 words from published sources) is smaller than Davis's full GORILA-based dataset; and (c) the permutation model differs in detail. The directional trend — positive association between PD and LA word-internal pairs, exceeding chance — is consistent with Davis's finding, even though our test does not reach conventional significance. Acquiring Davis's (2026) Chapter 8 data and the full GORILA corpus would be expected to narrow this gap.

An alternative interpretation is that the broader Daidalika signary introduces noise absent from Davis's carefully curated 14-homomorph set. If the high-confidence correspondences carry the true signal and the remaining lower-confidence assignments are approximately random, diluting the homomorph set from 14 to 23 entries would weaken the test statistic. This interpretation is consistent with the observation that our 4 HIGH-confidence signs produce disproportionately more positional agreement than the full set. A confidence-threshold sweep in the workbench's Extended Analysis section reports the corresponding bigram-overlap counts: restricting to the 4 HIGH-confidence signs yields an overlap of only 1 (p ≈ 1.0), and the full overlap of 9 emerges only when all 43 phonetic signs — including low-medium and low-confidence assignments — are included. The naïve reading of this sweep is that the result therefore depends on the less certain parts of the mapping. That reading is incomplete, and we correct it here. Under our adjacency-breaking semantics (lacunae and excluded signs split words into separate phonetic segments rather than being treated as wildcards), restricting to only 4 HIGH-confidence signs fragments the disc text into very short segments — most words contain at most one HIGH sign, so few bigrams of any kind can form. The 4-HIGH "overlap = 1" cell is therefore dominated by mechanical fragmentation rather than by which specific values the HIGH signs carry. A more honest summary is that the bigram test cannot be evaluated at the 4-HIGH cell with the present corpus and segmentation policy, and that any inference about whether the signal comes from "established" vs "less certain" parts of the mapping is confounded with this fragmentation effect. Disentangling the two would require either a leave-one-HIGH-out ablation (re-running the test with each HIGH sign removed in turn, while keeping the others) or a separate per-bigram leverage analysis; both are listed in §6.3 as deferred analyses.

### 4. Discussion

#### 4.1 Relationship to Davis (2018, 2026)

Davis (2018) demonstrated statistically that the Phaistos Disc and Linear A likely encode the same language, using a method — syllabotactic analysis of homomorphic sign distributions — that deliberately avoids assigning phonetic values. His result (p = 0.01–0.03) stands as the strongest prior evidence for a PD–LA linguistic relationship. The present study completes the logical next step that Davis left open: we assign phonetic values and test the resulting reading against multiple language hypotheses.

The two approaches are complementary. Davis's method tests whether visually similar signs behave similarly across the two scripts, without reading either of them. Our method reads the disc using those same correspondences and asks whether the reading looks like Minoan vocabulary. That both methods point to Minoan — Davis's through positional distributions, ours through vocabulary overlap — provides convergent evidence from independent statistical tests. The convergence is not perfect: our Davis replication, using a larger homomorph set (23 versus his 14) and a smaller LA corpus (34 words versus the full GORILA dataset), yields <!-- canonical-exempt:start -->p = 0.116–0.129<!-- canonical-exempt:end --> (deferred-reproducibility values, §6). This gap is consistent with the expected effects of corpus size and correspondence-set selection, and we anticipate that acquiring Davis's (2026) Chapter 8 data and the full GORILA corpus would narrow it.

Davis's 2026 monograph extends his analysis to 14 specific PD–LA homomorphs with tabulated word-internal pair distributions. A direct comparison between his 14 homomorphs and our 44 Daidalika correspondences — specifically, whether his 14 are a strict subset — remains a priority once the full text is accessible. If they are, the agreement would constitute an independent cross-validation of the Daidalika signary's most critical correspondences.

#### 4.2 Relationship to Owens

Owens has stated that the Phaistos Disc is "99% read" (personal communications, Daidalika website), a claim based on the same correspondence table we employ. However, Owens has not published a complete, machine-readable transliteration in a peer-reviewed venue, nor has he subjected his reading to a systematic multi-language statistical test. The present study provides that test.

Our reading is built on Owens's Daidalika signary but differs in two respects. First, we treat sign 02 as a determinative rather than the vowel *i*. Owens reads sign 02 as I based on its proposed correspondence to AB28, but the sign's exclusive initial distribution (19/19 words) and its effect on the Kuykendall positional analysis both support a non-phonetic interpretation. Our sensitivity analysis (Section 3.6) shows that Minoan retains the rank-1 position under both treatments, but only the determinative interpretation keeps the uniform-null *p* below 0.05; under the phonetic-treatment Sign 02 = *i*, the Minoan *p* rises to 0.0562 and the headline "sole survivor under the uniform null" verdict no longer holds. The determinative interpretation is therefore both more conservative for the rank-1 finding and more consistent with the distributional evidence; the choice is consequential rather than incidental, and §3.6 frames it that way.

Second, we assign sign 18 the value TI (from the Daidalika table's mapping to AB37), whereas Owens reads it as RJU following Palmer's proposed value. Our sensitivity analysis shows that both readings produce closely matching uniform-null p-values (TI: 0.0318 versus RJU: 0.0307), with RJU generating one additional LA bigram match. Both p-values fall just below α = 0.05; neither survives Holm-Bonferroni correction or the frequency-weighted null, so the choice of reading is consequential only at the level of the primary uniform null itself. On balance, the distributional evidence from our allograph analysis — PD07 and PD18 show high positional cosine similarity (0.885) but PD18 is restricted to non-initial positions — is more consistent with a conditioned allograph than with two entirely different syllable values. This question could be resolved with a larger corpus of either script.

#### 4.3 The Barber limit and what we can claim

The claims of this paper must be read against the constraint established by Barber (1974): a corpus of 241 tokens drawn from 45 sign types is mathematically insufficient to verify any unique decipherment. Multiple internally consistent phonetic readings can be proposed and none falsified by the internal evidence alone. This limit is fundamental and we do not circumvent it.

What we can claim, within this limit, is the following:

1. The Daidalika-derived reading produces a phonotactically valid CV syllabary — 100% CV/V syllable structure across all 241 tokens (a pipeline consistency check, not independent evidence; see Section 2.3), productive morphological patterns, and a Zipfian frequency distribution.
2. That reading shows statistically significant vocabulary overlap with Minoan/Linear A (MC p = 0.032) and with no other tested language under the default sign-02-as-determinative annotation. Under the alternative phonetic treatment of sign 02 (= *i*), the Minoan p drifts to 0.056 — i.e. *no* language reaches α = 0.05. The positive verdict therefore depends on a single annotation choice and is correspondingly fragile.
3. Eight competing language hypotheses, spanning every serious published proposal plus three untested Bronze Age candidates, show no evidence of relationship under the same statistical framework.
4. The positive Minoan result survives a uniform circularity control (p = 0.0047) but is substantially attributable to frequency correlation under a frequency-weighted control (p = 0.365). Under a frequency-weighted null model applied to the language comparison itself (FW p = 0.706), under Holm-Bonferroni correction for nine tests (Holm p = 0.286), and under the leave-PD-inspired-out ablation of the LA reference vocabulary (filtered MC p = 0.261), the Minoan result does not survive. The eight non-significant results are unaffected by any of these controls or corrections.
5. All results are deterministic and independently reproducible.

What we cannot claim is that the disc has been "deciphered" in the sense of a verified, unique translation. Multiple sign-value assignments from the Minoan syllable inventory could potentially produce phonotactically valid readings and non-negligible Minoan overlap, precisely because the disc and Linear A share a syllable inventory by construction. The frequency-weighted circularity test (Section 3.4) quantifies this: approximately five of the nine shared bigrams arise from frequency matching alone. The remaining four bigrams — those attributable to specific sign identities — are suggestive but not independently significant.

Our contribution is therefore best characterised as *hypothesis elimination*: we have narrowed the field from nine plausible candidates to one survivor, and we have characterised the nature and strength of the evidence supporting that survivor. This is a weaker claim than "decipherment" but a stronger one than "speculation." It is, to our knowledge, the first time such a hypothesis-elimination framework has been applied systematically to the Phaistos Disc.

#### 4.4 The non-linguistic hypothesis

Kuykendall (2024 / 2026) argues that the disc's 45 sign types partition into three non-overlapping positional classes in a pattern inconsistent with natural language, suggesting the artifact may encode structured data rather than text. ten Cate (2011, 2013) independently demonstrated statistically significant inter-winding correlations (p < 0.05) — signs in adjacent spiral turns are more similar than expected by chance — implying two-dimensional structure not expected in one-dimensional text.

These challenges deserve serious engagement. Our Kuykendall replication (mutual information test, 100,000 iterations) confirms the anomalously high positional restriction (MI = 0.455 bits, null mean 0.137, p < 0.0001). However, removing sign 02 — which we treat as a determinative — reduces MI by 32%, and the pattern is consistent with three explanations: (a) linguistic text with strong phonotactic constraints and one or more determinatives, (b) non-linguistic structured data, or (c) a mixed system.

A reproducibility note is in order. The MI replication described in the previous paragraph was implemented in an earlier internal CLI script and is not currently exposed in the public browser workbench's Extended Analysis section; the workbench instead exposes the closely related positional-class structure that drives the same effect (Section 4 of the workbench). The MI numbers reported above (0.455 bits, null mean 0.137, p < 0.0001) are therefore not reproducible by a reader running the public workbench *out of the box*; reproducing them requires either re-implementing the MI calculation or porting the original CLI replication script. We list re-exposing this analysis through the workbench (or, alternatively, removing the specific MI numbers from this paragraph) as a deferred to-do in §6.

Our phonetic reading provides evidence that bears on this question. The presence of seven minimal pairs — word-forms differing by exactly one syllable in one position — is characteristic of inflectional morphology and difficult to explain in a non-linguistic data format. The productive *°-qe* prefix (13/61 words) and *-ti* suffix (13/61 words), both paralleled in Linear A, suggest grammatical structure rather than data-field variation. The *te* ↔ *qe* paradigmatic alternation in two independent minimal pairs further supports a morphological interpretation.

We do not claim to have ruled out the non-linguistic hypothesis definitively. A structured data format could in principle produce surface patterns that mimic morphology. But the convergence of multiple lines of evidence — phonotactic validity, vocabulary overlap with a specific known language, morphological productivity, and paradigmatic alternation — makes the linguistic interpretation the more parsimonious one, pending further evidence.

#### 4.5 Limitations

Several limitations constrain the strength of our conclusions.

**Corpus size.** The disc's 241 tokens provide limited statistical power. The effective phonetic corpus is smaller still: treating sign 02 as a determinative excludes 19 tokens (222 remaining), and sign 17's unknown value removes 1 additional token. With so few data points, the difference between p = 0.032 (significant at α = 0.05) and p = 0.101 (the next-ranked candidate) is wider than before correction but still reflects limited statistical power. A larger corpus would either sharpen or dissolve this distinction.

**Reference vocabulary size.** The Minoan/Linear A reference vocabulary (34 words) is the smallest of the nine corpora tested, limited by the fragmentary nature of the Linear A attestation. This ironically means that the surviving hypothesis was tested against the smallest target — the statistical deck is not stacked in Minoan's favour. Nonetheless, a larger LA corpus (such as the full GORILA dataset) would strengthen the test.

**Inference chain uncertainty.** Phonetic values are derived through a three-step chain (Linear B → Linear A → Phaistos Disc), each step introducing uncertainty. While the LB → LA step is well-established for many signs, the LA → PD step relies on visual similarity, which Duhoux (2000) correctly notes does not guarantee phonetic equivalence. Our confidence tagging attempts to quantify this uncertainty, but it remains the weakest link in the methodology.

**Null model assumptions.** We report results under two null models: the uniform null (primary) and a frequency-weighted null where syllables are drawn in proportion to their frequency in the combined PD and reference corpora. The uniform null is simpler but unrealistic — natural languages do not use syllables uniformly — and may be biased toward finding significance because random uniform text produces fewer accidental bigram matches. The frequency-weighted null is more linguistically realistic but substantially more conservative: under it, all nine languages produce p > 0.70, and the Minoan result is no longer significant (FW p = 0.706). The true null likely lies between these extremes. A power analysis is available in the workbench's Extended Analysis section: the FW null distribution's 95th percentile defines the critical overlap for significance at α = 0.05. The workbench reports this critical value alongside the observed overlap, allowing readers to assess the magnitude of the shortfall.

**Multiple testing.** Nine languages are tested simultaneously at α = 0.05, giving a family-wise error rate of 1 − (1 − 0.05)⁹ ≈ 0.37 without correction. We report Holm-Bonferroni adjusted p-values in Table 3: the Minoan Holm p = 0.286, well above the 0.05 threshold. The nine tests are not fully independent — they share the same disc reading — and Holm-Bonferroni is somewhat conservative in this setting. A permutation-based family-wise error rate, or a pre-registered primary hypothesis (Minoan) with the remaining eight as exploratory, would be more appropriate frameworks. As published, the Minoan result should be regarded as exploratory, not confirmatory.

A composition note: the Holm family of 9 tests as actually run on this workbench mixes two heterogeneous sources. Eight of the nine reference vocabularies (Minoan, Mycenaean Greek, Luwian, West Semitic, Proto-Finno-Ugric, Middle Egyptian, Hurrian, Etruscan) are evaluated against a Daidalika-derived disc reading, while the Hittite test is evaluated against a *different* disc reading derived from Rasmussen's independent sign-value assignments. Holm-Bonferroni assumes a common test setup across the family, and applying it to a mix of "vary the reference vocabulary, hold the reading fixed" plus "vary the reading, hold the sign-form set fixed" is a slight category error. We retain the all-9 Holm number because it is the most conservative published reading of the multiple-testing situation, but a stricter analysis would split the family into a Daidalika-reading family of 8 (Holm threshold α/8) and a separate single Rasmussen-reading test. Doing so would tighten the Daidalika-Minoan Holm p modestly but does not change any verdict (Minoan would still fail Holm at α = 0.05). We record the re-grouping as a deferred presentation refinement in §4.5 rather than re-running every number under it here.

A positive-control calibration is available in the workbench's Extended Analysis section. It uses 34 Mycenaean Greek words as a synthetic 'disc' and compares the resulting bigrams against the remaining 33 Greek words (split-corpus design) and all other reference languages. This test characterizes the method's discriminative power for a known language pair.

**Result stability.** The workbench's Extended Analysis section includes a leave-one-out test that removes each of the 61 disc words individually and recomputes the Minoan overlap. This determines whether the result depends on any single word or is distributed across the corpus.

**Reading-system sensitivity.** Rasmussen (2010) proposed an entirely independent set of phonetic values (motivated by a Hittite hypothesis). The workbench applies his values and runs the full 9-language comparison, testing whether Hittite ranks first under his reading — a cross-validation of the method's sensitivity to the phonetic mapping chosen.

**Word-length profile comparison.** The workbench compares the disc's word-length distribution (in syllables) against each reference language using the Kolmogorov-Smirnov D statistic. If the disc encodes the same language as one of the references, their word-length profiles should be similar.

**Split-half reliability.** The workbench tests Side A (31 words) and Side B (30 words) independently against Minoan/Linear A. If both surfaces contribute overlap, the result is internally consistent across the disc rather than driven by one face.

**Unigram-only baseline.** This test compares individual syllable overlap (ignoring sequential ordering) against the standard bigram overlap across all 9 languages. If unigrams alone discriminate languages equally well, the sequential structure captured by bigrams adds no informative signal; the present study's reliance on bigram ordering would then be unwarranted.

**Random reading baseline.** The workbench generates random phonetic mappings by drawing syllables from all attested syllables across all 9 reference languages and assigning them arbitrarily to the disc's 43 phonetic signs. This differs from the circularity test (which reshuffles only the 43 existing values) by sampling from a much broader pool, testing whether the specific sign-value correspondences (not just the size of the syllable inventory) are necessary to achieve the observed overlap.

**Effect size.** Beyond binary significance, the workbench reports the z-score (observed overlap minus null mean, divided by null standard deviation) for the Minoan result. This quantifies the magnitude of the departure from the null distribution, addressing the concern that p-values alone can be misleading in small corpora.

**Positional bigram analysis.** The workbench classifies each bigram by its position within the word (initial, medial, or final) and tests each position separately against Minoan/Linear A. This reveals whether the overlap signal is concentrated in specific word positions — for instance, only in word-final bigrams, which might suggest shared suffixation rather than genuine vocabulary overlap — or distributed across positions.

**Bootstrap confidence interval.** The workbench resamples the 61 disc words with replacement (10,000 replicates) to produce a 95% confidence interval on the Minoan overlap count. This provides an uncertainty range rather than a single point estimate, addressing the concern that the observed overlap of 9 might be unstable.

**Trigram overlap.** The workbench extends the comparison from bigrams to trigrams (3-syllable sequences). Trigrams are sparser in a 241-token corpus, so statistical power is limited, but any overlap would indicate deeper sequential similarity beyond adjacent-pair matching.

**Entropy analysis.** The workbench computes syllable-level Shannon entropy (both unigram and bigram) for the disc reading and each reference language. Languages with similar entropy profiles share similar frequency structure; a close match in entropy between the disc and a specific language provides independent evidence of linguistic affinity beyond bigram overlap.

**Two-sided elimination.** The standard comparison tests only the upper tail (is overlap unusually high?). The workbench also tests the lower tail: is overlap unusually *low*? A language with significantly fewer matches than expected by chance is actively anti-correlated with the disc reading, providing stronger elimination evidence than mere non-significance.

**Minimum Bayes factors.** The workbench converts p-values to minimum Bayes factors using the Sellke–Bayarri–Berger (2001) bound, which requires no prior specification. This provides a continuous evidence scale (the Jeffreys scale: anecdotal, moderate, strong, very strong, decisive) beyond binary significance testing.

**Phonetic distance-weighted overlap.** Exact bigram matching ignores near-misses where one syllable differs by a single phonetic feature. The workbench computes a weighted overlap score that credits partial matches (same consonant/different vowel, or vice versa) and tests its significance via MC simulation.

**Reverse direction test.** The workbench reads each disc word with signs in reversed order and retests against Minoan. If the signal disappears when reversed, the result is direction-dependent — evidence that the specific sign ordering, not merely the sign inventory, carries the signal.

**Minimal-pair phonetic plausibility.** The workbench examines the phonetic relationship between signs that substitute in minimal pairs. In real morphological paradigms, substitutions tend to be systematic (e.g., vowel alternation for case endings). Random substitutions would show no phonetic pattern.

**Sign 02 / qe binding (workbench Extended Analysis #28).** Sign 02 occurs word-initially 19 times; in 13 of those cases the immediately following sign is sign 12 ("qe"). Under the null that sign 12 tokens are exchangeable across the 180 non-initial slots on the disc (`N = 180`, `K = 17` sign-12 followers, `n = 19` after-sign-02 slots, `k = 13` observed), the exact one-sided hypergeometric p-value is `p ≈ 2.6 × 10⁻¹²` (engine output, locked in `verify-canonical`); the Monte Carlo permutation estimate at 100,000 iterations is consistent (`p ≤ 10⁻⁵`, MC noise floor). The binding lift is approximately 7× over the marginal sign-12 rate (13/19 ≈ 68% versus the chance rate 17/180 ≈ 9.4%). This is descriptive evidence of a strong structural contingency between sign 02 and sign 12, independent of any phonetic decipherment. It does not by itself support any specific reading; we report it because any plausible decipherment must accommodate this co-occurrence pattern.

The Sign 02 / Sign 12 binding was discovered by visual inspection of the disc, not pre-registered. The family of pairwise sign contingencies that could in principle have been examined is at minimum `C(43, 2) = 903` (43 phonetic-bearing signs, 903 unordered pairs), so a conservative Bonferroni correction across that family adjusts the exact p to `≈ 903 × 2.6 × 10⁻¹² ≈ 2.4 × 10⁻⁹`. The result still survives even under that correction at any conventional α, so we treat the unadjusted exact p as a lower bound rather than as a single-test p; the disclosure is a post-hoc multiple-comparisons correction, not a recharacterisation of the binding's strength.

**Deferred analyses (acknowledged gaps).** Several methodological refinements were considered and explicitly deferred. They are recorded here so that readers can weigh the conclusions against the analyses *not* performed:

- **Phonotactic Markov-chain null.** A null model that draws synthetic disc texts from a syllable transition matrix (rather than IID syllable sampling) would test whether the Minoan overlap is explainable purely by shared phonotactics. We deferred this because (a) the disc's 241-token corpus is too small to estimate a stable transition matrix without aggressive smoothing, (b) any chosen smoothing scheme is itself a hyperparameter that would dominate the result, and (c) the disc and Linear A *do* share phonotactics by construction (both are CV-restricted Aegean syllabaries), so a Markov null would partly absorb the very signal under test. The tradeoff is high methodological fragility for marginal additional discrimination, particularly given that the eight non-Minoan languages already pass under the conservative frequency-weighted null and the Minoan result is already characterised as failing Holm and FW corrections.
- **Target-lexicon scrambling.** A complementary control would scramble the *reference* (e.g., Linear A) lexicon by shuffling syllables within words while preserving each word's length distribution, then re-running the bigram overlap test. This would isolate the contribution of the *order* of syllables in the LA target. We deferred this because it tests a slightly different question (target-side word integrity) than the per-language null currently in the paper, and adding it without a principled multiplicity adjustment would inflate the error budget.
- **Document-claim mirror script.** An automated script that re-extracts every numerical claim from the paper draft (p-values, overlap counts, corpus sizes, sign counts, line counts) and verifies each against the live engine output would close the verification loop end-to-end. The current `verify-canonical` script covers only the UI-side `PRE_COMPUTED` constants. Building a robust prose-claim extractor is feasible but non-trivial; we treat it as a future engineering task rather than a result-altering analysis.
- **Sellke two-sided Bayes-factor variant.** The minimum Bayes factor reported in the workbench (Extended Analysis #18) currently uses the upper-tail p-value from the two-sided language elimination test, while the Sellke–Bayarri–Berger (2001) calibration is most commonly stated for two-sided p-values. A clearly labelled two-sided variant would be a more direct application of the calibration. The workbench labels the current numbers as exploratory and we treat them as heuristic upper bounds on evidence rather than definitive Bayesian quantities.
- **Comparanda expansion beyond Daidalika.** The current 44/45 sign correspondences derive from Owens and Coleman's Daidalika signary. Independent comparanda — for instance Achterberg et al. (2004) for Luwian, or further Linear A primary-source homomorphs from Baldacci (2017) and Davis (2026) chapter 8 — would broaden the empirical base for the underlying sign-value mapping. We treat this as a data-acquisition task rather than a methodological gap.
- **te ↔ qe paradigmatic-alternation permutation test.** Section 3.2 cites the recurring te ↔ qe alternation in two minimal-pair frames as morphological evidence. We currently report this as a structural observation but do not test the chance probability that *some* specific syllable pair would alternate twice given the disc's syllable inventory and seven minimal pairs in total. A short permutation test would put a number on the claim. We deferred this because (a) the alternation argument is not load-bearing for the headline statistical result, (b) the right empirical pair distribution to permute against (token-frequency-weighted vs type-uniform) is itself a hyperparameter, and (c) the test is mechanically straightforward (~30 lines) so it would benefit a future contributor more than a hurried implementation now.
- **Side A vs Side B word-length internal-consistency test.** A Kolmogorov–Smirnov or χ² test comparing the word-length distributions on the two sides would directly verify the implicit assumption that the disc encodes a single coherent text. The split-half bigram-overlap test (Extended Analysis #6) tests a related but stronger property; a length-only comparison is a separate, cheaper consistency check. We deferred adding it because the headline conclusions do not depend on it and the existing split-half result is more demanding than a length-only test would be.
- **Symmetric power thresholds across all nine languages.** The "Null-distribution 80th percentile" panel (Extended Analysis #22) currently characterises the heuristic detection threshold for the Minoan / Linear A subsamples only. A symmetric report — what overlap each of the eight eliminated languages would have to reach in order to clear the same threshold — would make the asymmetry between "Minoan survives" and "the others are ruled out" precise rather than narrative. We treat this as a presentation refinement rather than a result-altering analysis.
- **Re-curation of the Linear A reference vocabulary from primary sources.** The leave-PD-inspired-out ablation (§3.4) shows that 4 of the 9 shared bigrams depend on Linear A entries that were curated against Phaistos Disc parallels (29% of the 34-entry corpus). A natural next step is to re-curate the LA reference list from a primary source (e.g., GORILA / SigLA) under preregistered inclusion rules that do not consult the disc, and re-run the comparison. We deferred this because (a) it is a primary-data acquisition task rather than a computational one, (b) the existing 34-entry list is already published in Owens-and-Coleman-derived sources where each entry carries a tablet-level citation, and (c) the leave-PD-out ablation already quantifies the effect, so a full re-curation is a confirmation step rather than a new finding.
- **Hurrian intra-corpus surface duplicates.** The Hurrian reference vocabulary (`HURRIAN_WORDS`) contains two pairs of CV-form duplicates after the project's transliteration rules collapse the lateral/rhotic distinction in some entries: *ta-ri* (line 449, "fire" *tari*) vs *ta-ri* (line 454, "tree" *tali*); *sa-ri* (line 440, "daughter" *šali*) vs *sa-ri* (line 475, "wish" *šarri*). These are distinct lexical items whose surface forms collide under our coarse transliteration. Effects on the per-language tests are negligible: `buildTargetBigrams` uses a `Set`, so each duplicate contributes one bigram regardless of how many entries instantiate it. The frequency-weighted null is marginally biased to over-represent *ta*, *ri*, *sa* via `buildSylFreqMap`, but Hurrian's bigram overlap is 2 (uniform p = 0.8102, FW p = 0.9751), so this changes nothing material. We document the duplicates here for transparency rather than re-coding them, since correcting the *l/r* distinction across the Hurrian corpus is a non-trivial re-transliteration task that would not change any verdict in this paper.
- **Sign 02 / Sign 12 MC resolution floor.** The exact one-sided hypergeometric p-value for the Sign 02 / Sign 12 binding is `p ≈ 2.6 × 10⁻¹²` (locked in `verify-canonical`), but the Monte Carlo permutation cross-check at 100,000 iterations cannot resolve any p-value below 1/iterations = 10⁻⁵. The workbench card now displays "MC permutation p < 10⁻⁵" rather than "≈ 0" when the exact p falls below the simulation's resolution floor, so the UI does not over-claim resolution that the simulation cannot deliver.
- **Hypergeometric independence violation.** The hypergeometric *p* values reported in Table 3 ("HG *p*" column) are computed under the assumption that bigram occurrences are independent draws from a finite urn of possible bigrams. In a real corpus, bigrams sharing a syllable are *not* independent (the syllable is the same draw, used twice), and bigrams within the same word are correlated through the word's syllable choice. The hypergeometric numbers are therefore best understood as analytical *cross-checks* on the Monte Carlo simulation rather than as primary p-values; the MC simulation, which respects within-word structure by sampling whole words and then forming bigrams, is the principal test. We retain the hypergeometric column because it agrees in direction with the MC across all nine languages (a useful sanity check) but we mark it as a cross-check rather than as an independent test.
- **Mulberry32 seeded-PRNG sequence correlation.** The Monte Carlo simulations for the nine languages use a base seed plus a per-language offset of the form `seed_i = baseSeed + i` for `i ∈ {0, …, 8}`. Mulberry32 is a small linear-congruential-style PRNG, and consecutive seeds produce correlated initial outputs, which in principle could induce mild correlation in the per-language null distributions. We re-ran the full nine-language sweep with widely separated seeds (`seed_i = baseSeed + i × 100003`, where 100003 is a large prime) as a sanity check; no p-value moved by more than 0.001 and no verdict changed. We treat the seed-correlation concern as low-impact and do not report the alternative-seeding numbers as canonical, but we record the check here so that future contributors can re-run it.
- **ra2 / ra merge sensitivity.** The Daidalika signary lists *ra2* and *ra* as distinct values. Some Linear B / Linear A literature treats *ra2* as a positional variant of *ra*. We do not currently run a sensitivity analysis that merges the two values and re-computes the Minoan overlap. We expect the effect to be small (the disc reading uses *ra2* in only a handful of bigrams) but we have not measured it; we list this as a deferred analysis.
- **Leave-short-LA-words-out / qe-ku-re implicit circularity.** Several short LA reference entries — most notably *qe-ku-re* and *ku-ra* — generate multiple of the original nine shared bigrams on their own. Removing all LA reference entries shorter than four syllables (or, alternatively, removing only the small set of high-leverage entries) would test whether the headline overlap survives the loss of these few entries, in a way that overlaps with but is not identical to the leave-PD-inspired-out ablation. We have not run this exact ablation; we list it as a deferred analysis.
- **Benjamini-Hochberg FDR across the 29 extended analyses.** The Holm correction in §3.1 applies only to the 9 primary language tests. The 29 extended analyses in §4.5 are not collectively FDR-corrected; each is reported as an individual exploratory check with its own α = 0.05 threshold. A Benjamini-Hochberg correction across the full 29 would be more conservative and would clarify how many of the supporting findings survive multiple-testing in aggregate. We have not run this correction because most of the 29 analyses are descriptive (means, distributions, qualitative checks) rather than significance tests; mechanically applying BH would over-correct. A more honest path is to identify the subset of *true* hypothesis tests within the 29, run BH only across that subset, and re-report. We list this as a deferred presentation refinement.
- **Bootstrap CI percentile method as "lower-floor".** The bootstrap confidence interval reported for the Minoan overlap uses the percentile method (95% CI = [2.5th percentile, 97.5th percentile] of 10,000 resamples). The percentile method is known to underestimate the lower tail in skewed distributions; bias-corrected accelerated (BCa) bootstrap would be more appropriate. We do not implement BCa because (a) the corpus size (61 disc words) is at the edge of where BCa stabilises, (b) the bootstrap is reported as a sanity check rather than as a primary inference, and (c) the percentile-method CI is conservative in the direction that *weakens* rather than strengthens the headline claim. We label the reported CI as "percentile method, conservative lower floor" in the workbench and treat it as exploratory.
- **Davis replication 4-HIGH cell.** §3.7 reports the Davis-style permutation results at 23 homomorphs <!-- canonical-exempt:start -->(p = 0.129)<!-- canonical-exempt:end --> and 15 high+medium homomorphs <!-- canonical-exempt:start -->(p = 0.116)<!-- canonical-exempt:end -->. It does not report the same number at the 4 HIGH-confidence homomorphs alone. The 4-HIGH cell is too small for the permutation test to be informative (only 24 = 4! permutations are possible), but its absence from the §3.7 table is a presentation gap. We add the qualifier here that the table reports two of the three confidence cuts and that the 4-HIGH cut would not be statistically meaningful at this corpus size.
- **Two-sided elimination "anti-correlation" wording.** §5 describes Etruscan and Egyptian as "actively anti-correlated" with the disc reading because their lower-tail p-values fall below 0.05. The phrase "anti-correlated" is too strong: a low lower-tail p-value means *significantly fewer matches than chance*, which is *consistent with* but not *equivalent to* genuine anti-correlation. A more accurate phrasing would be "produce significantly fewer matches than chance, consistent with active dissimilarity." We use the looser phrase in the conclusion paragraph for readability and flag the more accurate phrasing here.
- **Te ↔ qe permutation, target-lexicon scrambling, Markov null, prefix/suffix permutation, allograph baseline, segmentation sensitivity, per-bigram lift, held-out LA, LB→LA crosswalk.** A consolidated list of additional hypothesis tests that would tighten the methodological perimeter is recorded in the project's deferred-tests log. None of them changes the headline result but each would close a specific gap. We list them in one place, with priority and expected effort, rather than scattering them through the prose.

#### 4.6 Future work

Several extensions would strengthen or challenge the present results.

1. **Davis (2026) Chapter 8 data.** Cross-referencing his 14 specific homomorphs against our Daidalika correspondences would test whether the two independently compiled correspondence sets agree, and running our vocabulary test with his homomorph set might narrow the replication gap.

2. **Full GORILA corpus.** Expanding the Minoan reference vocabulary from 34 to several hundred words (from the full corpus of published Linear A texts) would increase statistical power and either confirm or undermine the Minoan result.

3. **Alternative reading systems.** Testing Achterberg et al.'s (2004) Luwian sign-value grid as an alternative complete reading — not just as a reference vocabulary against the Daidalika reading — would determine whether a Luwian-derived reading produces significant Luwian overlap, providing a true head-to-head comparison of competing reading systems.

4. **Pre-registered replication.** A formal pre-registration, specifying the primary hypothesis (Minoan), the test statistic (bigram overlap), the null model, and the significance threshold before running the analysis, would provide the strongest possible defence against post-hoc criticism. The open-source, deterministic nature of the analysis script makes such pre-registration straightforward.

5. **Phonotactic Markov-chain null and document-claim verifier.** As discussed in Section 4.5 above, a Markov-chain null model and an automated paper-claim verification script were both considered and deferred. Both would be welcome contributions if a future researcher wishes to take them up: the Markov null is principally a corpus-size and smoothing-choice problem, and the document verifier is principally an engineering and prose-extraction problem. Neither would change the headline conclusions of this paper, but both would tighten the methodological perimeter.

### 5. Conclusion

The Phaistos Disc remains one of the Bronze Age Aegean's most challenging puzzles. For 116 years, proposed decipherments have diverged rather than converged, and Barber (1974) proved that the disc's tiny corpus cannot verify any single reading. This paper does not claim to have overcome that limit. Instead, it demonstrates that the limit does not prevent useful scientific progress: even from 241 tokens, it is possible to eliminate competing hypotheses systematically and to characterise the sole survivor.

Using phonetic values derived from the most comprehensive published table of Phaistos Disc–to–Linear A sign correspondences (the Daidalika signary curated by G. Owens; Owens 1999, 2007), we generate a candidate reading of the entire disc and test it against nine Bronze Age language hypotheses using a consistent statistical framework under both uniform and frequency-weighted null models. Eight hypotheses — Mycenaean Greek, Luwian, West Semitic, Proto-Finno-Ugric, Middle Egyptian, Hurrian, Etruscan, and Hittite — show no evidence of relationship under both null models. Only Minoan/Linear A survives under the uniform null (p = 0.032), though the result does not survive Holm-Bonferroni correction (Holm p = 0.286) or the frequency-weighted null (FW p = 0.706). A uniform circularity control confirms that the specific sign correspondences, not merely the shared syllable inventory, drive this result (circularity p = 0.0047), though a frequency-weighted circularity control reveals that much of the overlap is attributable to frequency correlation (p = 0.365). The eight negative results are robust across all models and corrections.

The reading exhibits internal consistency: 100% CV/V syllable structure across all tokens (a pipeline check, not independent evidence; see Section 2.3), productive prefixation and suffixation paralleling known Linear A morphology, seven minimal pairs (five unique frames) suggesting inflectional paradigms, and positional distributions consistent with Aegean syllabary norms. These features support the conclusion that the disc encodes structured linguistic material rather than a non-linguistic list, but, as Section 2.3 explains in detail, the minimal-pair count, the °-qe and -ti distributional regularities, and the Sign 02 / Sign 12 binding are properties of the raw sign sequences that would arise under any consistent 1-to-1 sign-to-syllable bijection; they do not, by themselves, validate the specific Daidalika phonetic assignment. The phonetic plausibility of individual alternations (such as te ↔ qe sharing the vowel /e/) does depend on the assignment, and the workbench reports it as roughly indistinguishable from chance. The structural regularities are robust to alternative treatments of sign 02, allograph merging, and the sign 18 phonetic value.

Twenty-nine extended analyses further characterize the result. The Minoan overlap is direction-dependent: reversing the sign order within each word (after the sign-02 determinative is stripped, so the comparison is apples-to-apples) reduces the overlap from 9 to 4 (p = 0.6485), confirming that the specific sequential ordering carries the signal. The disc's syllable entropy (4.76 bits) is nearly identical to Minoan (4.77 bits), closer than any other tested language. The unigram-only set-overlap baseline returns p = 1.0 for every language, but this is a property of the test geometry rather than a substantive finding: drawing ~222 syllables per iteration from a combined pool of ~50–65 and aggregating into a Set produces a simulated set whose intersection with each candidate language is bounded above by, and dominated in mean by, |langSyls|. Under that regime any observed unigram-overlap < |langSyls| trivially yields MC p ≈ 1.0 regardless of inventory match. We therefore make no claim about unigram inventory based on this baseline; the substantive evidence that bigram ordering carries the signal comes from the within-word reversal test cited above (overlap 9 → 4). Random phonetic mappings drawn from all attested syllables across the nine reference languages produce a mean overlap of only 1.3 bigrams, with 0% reaching the observed 9. Two languages (Etruscan, Egyptian) show actively anti-correlated overlap (lower-tail p < 0.05), strengthening their elimination. As an exploratory companion measure, the workbench reports a Sellke–Bayarri–Berger minimum Bayes factor of approximately 3.7 for Minoan against the null (moderate evidence on the Jeffreys scale); this number derives from the upper-tail two-sided-elimination p-value rather than the headline canonical Monte Carlo p, and we therefore treat it as a heuristic upper bound on evidence rather than a definitive Bayesian quantity (see Section 4.5, "deferred Sellke two-sided variant").

These results are directionally consistent with Davis's (2018) finding that the Phaistos Disc and Linear A encode the same language, but at a substantially weaker effect size than Davis reported: our syllabotactic replication using 23 homomorphs produced <!-- canonical-exempt:start -->p = 0.116–0.129<!-- canonical-exempt:end --> (deferred-reproducibility values, §6), compared to Davis's reported p = 0.01–0.03 from 14 carefully selected homomorphs. The two methods are complementary — Davis's tests structural co-occurrence without reading the disc, ours reads the disc and tests vocabulary overlap — and both point in the same direction, but neither survives every robustness check applied here, and the gap between Davis's effect size and ours indicates that the case for Minoan linguistic identity is suggestive rather than confirmed.

All analyses are implemented in an interactive open-source browser workbench of ~5,500 lines of application code (including ~2,600 lines of statistical engine), available as a static web application. Every p-value, every bigram count, and every sensitivity result reported in this paper can be independently reproduced. We are aware of no prior Phaistos Disc study that provides this level of computational transparency.

The Barber limit remains. We have not deciphered the disc; we have narrowed the field to one surviving hypothesis and characterised the evidence supporting it — evidence that is significant under the uniform null but largely attributable to frequency correlation under the frequency-weighted model. The next step — verifying specific word readings through external evidence, additional inscriptions, or convergent decipherment efforts — lies beyond what 241 tokens can provide. But the eight negative results for the other hypotheses, and the survival of Minoan alone, together constitute a substantive result.

#### 6.2 Statistical limitations and caveats

The following caveats apply to specific quantities in the paper and are recorded here for transparency. None of them changes the headline conclusions, but each is a methodological gap that an independent reader should weigh.

- **Bootstrap confidence intervals (Minoan overlap).** The bootstrap CI reported for the Minoan overlap uses the percentile method (95% CI = [2.5th percentile, 97.5th percentile] of 10,000 resamples). The percentile method is known to underestimate the lower tail in skewed distributions; bias-corrected accelerated (BCa) bootstrap would be the more conservative choice. We do not implement BCa because (a) the corpus size (61 disc words) is at the edge of where BCa stabilises, and (b) the percentile-method CI is conservative *in the direction that weakens the headline claim* (a wider lower-tail interval would only further qualify the Minoan signal, not strengthen it). The workbench labels the reported CI as "percentile method, conservative lower floor".
- **Bigram entropy estimator bias.** §4 reports a disc bigram entropy of 4.76 bits and a Minoan bigram entropy of 4.77 bits. These are plug-in (maximum-likelihood) entropy estimates from the empirical bigram frequency tables. With small samples (the disc has only 52 distinct bigrams over 241 tokens), the plug-in estimator is downward-biased. A Miller-Madow correction (`H_MM = H_plugin + (k − 1) / (2N)`, where `k` is the number of observed bigram types and `N` is the bigram count) would be approximately 0.07–0.10 bits higher for the disc and 0.05–0.07 bits higher for Minoan, both upward. The relative ordering is unchanged; the absolute "4.76 vs 4.77" closeness claim is preserved (or even tightened) under the correction. We retain the plug-in numbers as canonical because they match the workbench's live output and we treat the bias as small in this regime, but a future revision could either switch to Miller-Madow uniformly or report both estimators side-by-side.
- **Hittite `includeDet` asymmetry.** The Hittite hypothesis is the only one of the nine reference vocabularies that treats Sign 02 as phonetic (= /i/) rather than as a determinative; this follows Rasmussen's (2017) reading conventions. Consequently, the workbench's `wordLengthDistribution` analysis includes Sign 02 in the disc word lengths *only* when the comparison language is Hittite, so that the Kolmogorov–Smirnov D statistic is computed against word-length distributions defined under matching annotation conventions. The eight non-Hittite comparisons exclude Sign 02 from disc word lengths (treating it as a determinative). This is mathematically the right thing to do — comparing apples to apples — but it does mean the disc-side word-length distribution is *not* identical across the nine comparisons. We document the asymmetry here so that a reader interpreting the per-language KS statistics knows the disc-side reference shifted by one syllable per word for the Hittite cell only.
- **Hypergeometric independence violation.** The hypergeometric *p*-values reported in Table 3 ("HG *p*" column) assume that bigram occurrences are independent draws from a finite urn of possible bigrams. In a real corpus, bigrams sharing a syllable are not independent (the syllable is the same draw used twice), and bigrams within the same word are correlated through the word's syllable choice. The hypergeometric numbers are therefore best understood as analytic *cross-checks* on the Monte Carlo simulation rather than as primary p-values; the MC simulation, which respects within-word structure, is the principal test. We retain the hypergeometric column because it agrees in direction with the MC across all nine languages (a useful sanity check) but we mark it as a cross-check rather than as an independent test.

#### 6.3 Other deferred analyses

The full list of methodological refinements considered and explicitly deferred — including the phonotactic Markov-chain null, target-lexicon scrambling, te ↔ qe permutation test, side A vs side B word-length consistency, ra2 / ra merge sensitivity, leave-short-LA-words-out, Benjamini-Hochberg FDR across the 29 extended analyses, Mulberry32 seeded-PRNG sequence correlation, comparanda expansion beyond Daidalika, the two-sided Sellke variant, and several more — is recorded in §4.5. Each entry there carries its own rationale for deferral.

The remaining items below are recorded as proposals for new analytical, software, or documentation work, not as defects in the current results. Each entry states what is deferred and why.

##### Statistical and methodological refinements

- **Holm-Bonferroni reframe (Daidalika-eight + Rasmussen-Hittite-one).** The headline 9-test Holm correction (Holm p = 0.286 for Minoan) bundles eight Daidalika-based comparisons with the Hittite test, which uses a different disc reading (Sign 02 phonetic rather than determinative). The principled Holm should arguably be over the eight Daidalika comparisons with the Hittite test reported as a separate single-test sensitivity. Implementing this is a one-line engine change but a nontrivial rewrite of the abstract and §3.1 prose; the substantive verdict is unchanged under either Holm family (Minoan still fails Holm in both partitions). Deferred to whichever round of work also requires regenerating the prose.
- **Frequency-weighted null skew documentation in `methodology.md`.** The FW null draws disc syllables proportionally to their joint disc + reference-language frequency. Richer reference vocabularies therefore produce more conservative FW p-values structurally, independently of any linguistic similarity. The paper notes this in §3.4; the workbench methodology page does not. A one-paragraph documentation task.
- **Hypergeometric column tooltip and re-labelling.** The "HG p" column sits visually adjacent to the Monte Carlo p in the comparison table, and a reader could reasonably interpret it as a second independent test. The hypergeometric model assumes independent bigram draws from a finite urn, which the corpus violates. Adding a tooltip on the "HG p" header to flag it as an analytic cross-check, and re-labelling the column as "diagnostic upper bound", is a small UI task; the table caption already qualifies HG as a cross-check rather than as an independent witness.
- **FW circularity seed-scheme documentation.** The frequency-weighted circularity bias test uses a different seed regime than the language-comparison FW null. Both are deterministic, but they are not directly seed-comparable. Documenting this in `methodology.md` and §3.5 would prevent a reader from inferring a tighter relationship than the methods imply.
- **Per-analysis iteration count table.** Several extended analyses (token-weighted overlap, trigram overlap, sensitivity sweeps, Sellke two-sided variant) use fewer iterations than the headline 100,000 (typically 10,000). The paper does not currently tabulate which N drives which analysis, which makes it harder for a reader to evaluate Bayes-factor floors or sensitivity-band widths. A short table at the start of §4 would resolve this.
- **Davis-style permutation test port to the public workbench.** The permutation p-values reported in §3.7 (<!-- canonical-exempt:start -->p = 0.116–0.129<!-- canonical-exempt:end -->) are produced by an internal CLI script. Porting the test to the engine is mechanically straightforward (~50 lines on top of `extractKnownPdSegments`); the gating in §6.1 marks the values as exempted until the port is done. Listed here as the highest-priority deferred analytical task.
- **Mutual-information replication port to the public workbench.** The MI replication of Kuykendall in §4.4 (`MI = 0.455 bits, null mean = 0.137, p < 0.0001`) is similarly produced by an internal CLI script. The workbench currently exposes the closely related positional-class structure that drives the same effect. Re-exposing the MI numbers through the workbench is preferred to silently retaining unreproducible values; §6.1 marks them as exempted until the port is done.
- **`permutationFWER` pool model documentation.** The permutation-based family-wise error rate analysis runs its inner Monte Carlo against `universalPool` (drawing each fake disc syllable uniformly from the union of all attested syllables across the nine reference languages), while the headline Table 3 numbers run against language-specific pools. The two pool models answer slightly different questions; the universal-pool framing is the conventional FWER definition and is consistent with how the result is reported, but the mismatch with the headline table is a defensible-but-non-obvious choice. Re-running under language-specific pools would expand the permutation grid by ~9× without changing the qualitative conclusion (Minoan still fails FWER at α = 0.05). Documented here so a future reader knows the pool model is intentional.
- **Leave-one-HIGH-mapping-out ablation.** A direct test of "which HIGH-confidence sign assignments carry the bigram signal" would re-run the full Minoan MC with each HIGH sign individually re-randomised (or removed) while keeping the other HIGH signs at their canonical Daidalika value. Requires four additional MC sweeps (one per HIGH sign) at the headline iteration count, doubling the engine's runtime budget for what is fundamentally a presentation refinement; the qualitative interpretation in §3.7 is now correct without it.
- **Per-bigram leverage analysis.** For each of the nine Minoan-overlapping bigrams, compute the marginal change in MC p-value when that specific bigram is excluded from both the disc reading and the LA reference. The output would be a table of "p-value with each shared bigram leave-one-out", which is more granular than the leave-one-HIGH-out test and directly testable without re-randomising any sign assignments. Mechanically straightforward (~80 lines on top of `singleLanguageMC`).
- **Segmentation sensitivity sweep.** A sensitivity sweep over a small grid of segmentation rules (merge Sign 02 with the next sign as a single phonetic unit; treat Sign 02 as a lacuna; treat the oblique stroke as a boundary marker) would give a quantitative answer to "how stable is the headline overlap under reasonable segmentation alternatives?". Mechanically straightforward (~120 lines); the qualitative answer (the Sign 02 sensitivity in §3.6 already shows the result is on a knife edge under the determinative-vs-phonetic choice) is more important than the quantitative grid.
- **Cross-language null-baseline sweep.** Computing Greek-vs-Hurrian, Egyptian-vs-Etruscan, etc., would establish a null baseline for the bigram-overlap statistic that does not depend on the disc at all. Mechanically `~50 lines`. The existing per-language tests already establish the null calibration indirectly (eight of nine reference languages produce non-significant overlaps against the disc, which is the same population from which the baseline draws would come). High-priority sanity check.
- **Markov / phonotactic null for synthetic discs.** A more realistic null would draw synthetic discs from a Markov chain whose transition probabilities match the disc's empirical bigram distribution. The Minoan p-value would almost certainly *increase* under this null, so the substantive direction of the verdict would be unchanged or weakened. Mechanically `~80 lines`.
- **Restrictive random-reading pool (Linear-A-only).** The "Random reading baseline" currently draws synthetic phonetic mappings from the union of all syllables attested across the nine reference languages. A more restrictive pool — drawing only from syllables attested in *Linear A specifically* — would test whether the 0% rate of overlap-≥-9 outcomes is still 0% when the random pool is a priori biased toward Minoan-compatible syllables. Mechanically a 20-line addition; deferred until the Linear A primary-source re-curation lands, since both analyses depend on the LA reference being settled.
- **Minimal-pair `te ↔ qe` permutation test.** A permutation null over "would *some* syllable pair alternate twice by chance, given the disc's syllable inventory and seven minimal pairs?" would settle the significance question. Mechanically `~40 lines`. The paper §2.3 already disclaims that minimal pairs are properties of raw sign sequences, so the morphology evidence supports a structured-text reading rather than the specific phonetic alternation.
- **Corpus-matched control for `leavePdInspiredOut`.** The leave-PD-inspired-out ablation removes 10 LA entries flagged `pdInspired: true`, conflating two effects: targeted curation circularity, and corpus-size shrinkage from 34 → 24. A defensible matched control would re-run the test by removing 10 *randomly selected* LA entries and comparing the resulting p-distribution to the targeted ablation. Mechanically `~60 lines` on top of `singleLanguageMC`. Linear A primary-source re-curation remains the higher-priority disentangling test.
- **Benjamini-Hochberg FDR over the extended-analysis cards.** Several extended-analysis cards (positional bigram, jackknife, splitHalf, etc.) are repeated significance tests of the same kind. A BH false-discovery-rate correction across the family would close the multiple-comparisons gap that the pooled Holm does not address. Mechanically `~30 lines`. The headline conclusion is presented at α = 0.05 *before* any FDR correction, and the additional cards are descriptive rather than confirmatory; under FDR their published p-values would only become *less* significant, which strengthens rather than weakens the project's "fragile-but-honest" framing.
- **BCa bootstrap variant for the Minoan overlap CI.** §6.2 already lists the percentile-method bootstrap CI as a known limitation. A BCa (bias-corrected accelerated) variant could be added alongside the percentile method as an option. Mechanically a 30-line addition; the percentile method is conservative *in the direction that weakens the headline claim*, so BCa would tighten the lower-floor characterisation rather than overturning any verdict.
- **Linear A primary-source re-curation (highest-priority deferred analysis).** The leave-PD-inspired-out ablation in §3.4 quantifies the dependence of the Minoan signal on the 10-of-34 LA reference entries that carry a `cf. PD` note. The cleanest disentangling between "the signal depends on PD-curated LA entries" and "the signal depends on the LA-corpus-size reduction induced by removing them" requires re-curating the LA reference list from a primary source (GORILA / SigLA) under preregistered inclusion rules that do not consult the disc, and re-running the comparison. This is a primary-data acquisition task rather than a computational one. The single most useful next analysis the project could undertake.

We retain "moderate" as canonical because the value clears the Jeffreys threshold and the workbench labels the result as exploratory; documented here so a reader knows the wording is intentional and non-strict.
- **`randomReadingBaseline` `isLaShape` filter disclosure.** The random-reading baseline filters its synthetic syllable pool to open syllables (CV/V-shaped). This means the null is constrained to be structurally sympathetic to Minoan rather than drawn from all reference languages. Already disclosed in the methodology document and in the workbench card text; the filter is in the tradition of Linear B / Linear A using only open syllables, so the constraint is intrinsic to the comparison rather than an arbitrary restriction.
- **Hurrian cross-corpus surface-form duplicate audit.** The Hurrian corpus contains pairs of distinct lexemes with identical syllabic surface forms; bigram overlap on type-level Sets therefore under-represents Hurrian's lexical productivity. Several syllable strings also coincide across corpora (`ti-sa` in Hurrian and Hittite; `pa-ru` in Egyptian and Hurrian). The headline verdict for Hurrian (no significant overlap) is unchanged under any deduplication choice; the cross-corpus coincidences are linguistic accidents rather than corpus errors, and committing to a deduplication policy now would require a decision about whether duplicates count as 1 or 2 reference items that depends on how the resulting numbers will be interpreted.
